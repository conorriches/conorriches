Teaching soldering with custom designed PCBee

Going to maker events

The rain was hammering on the windows at the hackspace one cold February evening in 2018, a few of us were huddled under the heat lamp working out what to do for MakerFaire and MakeFest.

We were determined to show up to these events as exhibitors - we just needed to find out what. It had to be hands on, something people would come to, and leave with something of value.

To Bee or Not to bee

The symbol of Manchester is the worker bee - a reflection back to the industrial revolution when Manchester was full of working mills and a hive of industreous activity. It still is, but the mills are now trendy flats and the industreous activity is usually over a latte or foosball table.

The bee emblem can be found all over the city, including on bollards, and it may well have been the inexplicable appearance of a bollard in the space that gave me the idea that we should do something featuring the bee.

So using the laser cutter, I made a quick bee and using an LED and battery, we had a prototype.

With this prototype, my mind was pretty fixed on laser cutting some template and having some way of people assembling and colouring in the bee.

However, the brilliant idea someone else had was to make a PCB - something I'd never done before and something which was way out of my skill league.

The idea was instantly attractive, a we wouldn't need to work out how to secure the electronics to the bee, and soldering is easy.

Making a PCB

I was very fortunate that a friend at the space was willing to teach me how to use KiCad software to design PCBs.

The idea was a hexagonal board, with the bee in the middle. It seemed easy enough with the help of someone experienced, but when I tried to do it alone I quickly got stuck! With someone knowledgable neaby, I found out about ENIG - using gold on the front of the board which could be used to make a beautiful effect.

I made a rough first design, and when this was checked over (turns out I'd shorted out a few bits, this would have made the battery become a small heater) and sorted out, we were ready to go ahead and get a small batch made by Elecrow.

We soldered some up, and they worked! It felt like we were really onto something here.

Refining and pricing

During testing we found that the hole in the middle, used to wear the board as a badge, interfered with the depth of the battery holder meaning it was tricky to wear it as a badge.

We moved the hole to the top of the badge, and then did something scary - order 500 badges!

We booked our place at Maker Faire and rallied a team together.

We also had to get parts:

  • LEDs, two per badge, plus some margin for error. We wanted a mix of colours so people could personalise their badge.
  • Battery holders
  • Batteries

These were all costed out in order for us to set a price point - we had NO idea what people would pay for them, if they would pay at all.

We figured that going for pocket money amounts would likely be successful, so we priced them at £4 for the kit, and £6 assembled - this way people who wanted to solder them could do so for under £5, but those that wanted to just buy one would pay a small premium.

Risk assessments done (namely "don't touch the shiny part of the soldering iron - you'll let go very quickly" and "don't eat the batteries") we were good to go.

Our attitude at this point was one of nerves and acceptance - we were totally okay if we didn't make our money back over the weekend. I know I defined a success as making our money back, and wouldn't be upset if there wasn't really the interest.

We were agreed that we could go as low as £3 if we had to, so with a cash float, boxes of leaflets and promotional stuff for the space (as well as projects and a monitor showing a slideshow), we packed our stuff, and headed for Newcastle.
